The problem

Did you know that the agriculture industry (primarily from pesticides and fertilizer) contributes to 10% of our global pollution? There is a 6,300 square mile dead zone in the Gulf of Mexico because fertilizer runoff depletes the oxygen in water and makes it impossible for marine life to survive. A dramatic increase in previously limited nutrients causes massive algal blooms that kill fish, poisons humans when they eat seafood, and the death of marine mammals and shore birds.

Manufacturing fertilizer leads to the release of harmful greenhouse gasses into the atmosphere and using fertilizer leads to oxygen depletion in our waterways. This eventually results in ocean acidification, altering marine food chains and food supply to humans. Acidification also decreases storm protection from reefs, tourism opportunities, access to food, oxygen production, and other benefits of the ocean that are invaluable.

Clean solutions

Alternatives to chemical fertilizer:

  • Compost is nature’s way of using its own resources to feed the next cycle of plant life. It truly is a zero waste way of gardening. It also is the most effective way, because instead of working against nature as chemical-based fertilizers often do, compost works alongside it - just as nature intends.
    Where to buy: Home Depot, Lowe’s
  • Lomi dirt is an all-natural alternative to fertilizer. Instead of feeding plants with synthetic compounds, Lomi dirt simply blends with the soil you already have, boosting its nutrient content organically.
    Where to buy:
    Where to buy: Pela
  • Manure has the ability to increase the carbon in soil while also decreasing the carbon in the atmosphere. Just make sure to only add composted manure to your garden, not fresh, as fresh manure can contain hormones, weed seeds, chemicals and harmful bacteria.
    Where to buy: Home Depot, Lowe's
  • Bat guano is a suitable fertilizer for plants and lawns, making them healthy and green. It can be used as a natural fungicide, and it controls nematodes in the soil as well. In addition, bat guano makes an acceptable compost activator, speeding up the decomposition process.
    Where to buy: Amazon
  • Worm castings have humus which helps increase soil's water retention, improve soil aeration and anchor plant nutrients that would otherwise leach away with water. Castings also feed beneficial soil microorganisms that produce, store and slowly release plant nutrients into soil to feed plants.
    Where to buy: Home Depot, Lowe's
  • Peat moss is an important component of most potting soils and seed starting mediums. It holds several times its weight in moisture, and releases the moisture to the plant's roots as needed. It also holds onto nutrients so that they aren't rinsed out of the soil when watering the plant. Additionally, it contains less than 1 percent nitrogen, with phosphorus and potassium below 0.1 percent.
    Where to buy: Home Depot, ACE Hardware, Lowe's

What to avoid:

  • Milorganite 6-4-0 Fertilizer
    • PFOA and PFOS, two of the most well-researched forms of the PFAS, were both detected.
  • Fertilizers containing nitrates and phosphorus
    • High levels of nitrates can be toxic to livestock and humans. Nitrates are not adsorbed to soil materials, so they may leach into groundwater. In some instances, stored or land-applied manures or nitrogen fertilizers have caused high concentrations of nitrates in water. Because nitrates freely leach down through the soil profile, nitrogen that is not used for crop or plant growth can reach the groundwater easily.
    • Too much phosphorus can cause increased growth of algae and large aquatic plants, which can result in decreased levels of dissolved oxygen– a process called eutrophication. High levels of phosphorus can also lead to algae blooms that produce algal toxins which can be harmful to human and animal health.

Number of Hypoxic Ecosystems Per U.S. State

A hypoxic ecosystem is defined as a dead zone where marine life is unable to survive due to levels of oxygen in the water being so low that it can’t support life. This is directly correlated with fertilizer runoff, which triggers the growth of algae blooms. During their decaying process, they use up almost all of the oxygen, leading to the death of other organisms in the ecosystem. According to this data, there are a total of 433 dead zones in the U.S., with the majority of them being concentrated in Florida and Texas.
